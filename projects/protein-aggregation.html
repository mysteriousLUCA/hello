Surfactant-Induced BSA Aggregation: Modulation by Small-Molecule Additives

Key takeaway

Glucose, mannitol, lactose, and MSG mitigate SDS-induced BSA aggregation to varying degrees, while free glutamic acid slightly exacerbates it. Aggregation collapses when pH approaches the BSA isoelectric point (~4.7).

Abstract

Protein–surfactant interactions govern formulation stability in biopharmaceuticals and food technology. Bovine serum albumin (BSA) serves as a proxy for the human plasma protein HSA, sharing homologous folding domains that undergo cooperative unfolding in the presence of the anionic surfactant sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) (1, 2). We quantified SDS-triggered BSA aggregation at pH 3.8 by optical density (OD₆₀₀ nm) and examined how five additives—glucose, mannitol, lactose, glutamic acid (GA), and monosodium glutamate (MSG)—alter aggregation profiles. OD₆₀₀ decreased linearly with increasing concentrations of the four neutral/osmotically active additives, indicating progressive disaggregation, whereas GA produced a slight OD₆₀₀ rise. An independent titration revealed precipitous OD₆₀₀ loss above pH 4.4, consistent with electrostatic repulsion near the BSA isoelectric point (3). These findings illuminate simple excipient strategies for controlling protein aggregation in acidic media (4, 5).

Introduction

Albumins bind a plethora of ligands and are particularly sensitive to amphiphilic perturbants such as SDS, which unfolds proteins via "bead-necklace" complexes that expose hydrophobic cores and promote inter-molecular association (1, 6). Aggregation hampers therapeutic efficacy and triggers immunogenicity; therefore, excipient screening remains a critical formulation step (4, 7). Sugars (e.g., glucose, lactose), polyols (mannitol), and amino-acids/glutamate salts are frequently employed stabilizers, acting through preferential exclusion, water structuring, or charge shielding mechanisms (4, 8). This study evaluates each additive's capacity to counteract SDS-induced aggregation of BSA under acidic conditions representative of some parenteral formulations.

Materials and Methods

Reagents and Equipment

• BSA (Sigma-Aldrich, ≥98%)
• SDS (Bio-Rad, electrophoresis grade)
• Additives: d-glucose, d-mannitol, lactose monohydrate, l-glutamic acid, monosodium glutamate
• 0.05 M sodium acetate buffer, pH 3.8 (±0.02)
• UV-Visible spectrophotometer (λ = 600 nm, 1 cm pathlength cuvette)

Experimental Design

1. In six 3 mL test tubes per additive, buffer volumes were adjusted to maintain constant final volume (3 mL) after titrating BSA (160 µL, 1 mM), SDS (400 µL, 100 mM), and additive stock.
2. Additive concentrations ranged from 0→2.44 M (glucose), 0→1 M (mannitol), 0→400 mM (lactose), 0→25 mM (GA), and 0→50 mM (MSG).
3. pH titration employed identical BSA/SDS mixture adjusted with HCl or NaOH (pH 3.6–4.8).

Data Analysis

Triplicate readings were averaged; linear regression assessed additive efficacy (slope magnitude reflects disaggregation strength). Graphs were generated via Chart.js for clarity.

Results and Discussion

Glucose Effect

A steep OD₆₀₀ decline from 2.122→0.010 across 0–2.44 M glucose indicates potent aggregation inhibition, consistent with sugar-mediated preferential hydration stabilizing the native-like monomer (4).

Mannitol Effect

Mannitol achieved moderate OD₆₀₀ reduction (1.974→1.559, 0–1 M). Its lower efficacy relative to glucose likely stems from weaker kosmotropic water structuring and higher solution viscosity limiting molecular crowding benefits (9).

Lactose Effect

Lactose decreased OD₆₀₀ only slightly (2.148→1.920, 0–400 mM), reflecting steric constraints of disaccharide size and limited solubility under acidic pH (8).

Glutamic Acid vs MSG

GA slightly increased OD₆₀₀ (2.011→2.089, 0–25 mM), whereas MSG decreased it (2.011→1.201, 0–50 mM), highlighting the critical role of sodium counterion in modulating protein-surfactant interactions through charge screening.

pH Dependence

OD₆₀₀ remained high (≥1.96) below pH 4.4 but collapsed to zero at pH 4.6-4.8, aligning with the BSA isoelectric point where net charge neutrality minimizes intermolecular attraction and SDS binding affinity (3, 10).

Mechanistic Insights

Additive efficacy correlates with (i) osmolyte-driven preferential exclusion (glucose > mannitol > lactose), (ii) ionic repulsion (MSG), and (iii) pH-mediated charge modulation (near-pI disaggregation). SDS forms micellar strings along the polypeptide backbone, exposing hydrophobes that aggregate unless counteracted by stabilizers (1, 11).

Applications and Future Directions

The findings from this study have several practical applications in biopharmaceutical formulation and food technology:

1. Therapeutic Protein Formulations – Glucose and MSG can be incorporated as stabilizing excipients in acidic protein formulations to prevent aggregation-induced immunogenicity.

2. Food Processing – Understanding surfactant-protein interactions can improve texture and stability of protein-rich food products.

3. Analytical Methods – The optical density approach provides a simple, high-throughput screening method for excipient efficacy.

Future research directions include:

• Investigating synergistic effects between different excipient classes
• Extending the study to therapeutic proteins beyond albumin
• Developing predictive models for excipient selection based on protein properties
• Exploring the molecular mechanisms of stabilization using advanced biophysical techniques

Conclusions

1. Glucose and MSG are the most effective excipients, reducing BSA aggregate turbidity by >40% at moderate concentrations.
2. Mannitol and lactose confer partial protection, whereas free glutamic acid may worsen aggregation.
3. pH control near the albumin isoelectric point abolishes SDS-driven aggregation, offering a complementary stabilization route.
4. These findings provide practical guidance for formulation scientists working with protein-based products in acidic environments.
