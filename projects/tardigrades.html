Isolation and Characterization of Novel Tardigrades Around the Central Region of India

Key takeaway

This winter internship at IISER Bhopal successfully documented novel tardigrade species from the underexplored central region of India using Foldscope portable microscopy and standardized sampling protocols, contributing significantly to Indian tardigrade biodiversity research and establishing methodological frameworks for future systematic surveys.

Abstract

This winter internship aimed to uncover novel species of tardigrades from ponds, lichens, and mosses across the Bhopal region under the supervision of Dr. Nagarjun Vijay at the Indian Institute of Science Education and Research, Bhopal. The study utilized Foldscope portable microscopy for field observations and systematic sampling of diverse microhabitats including pond water, lichens, tree bark, moss, and leaf litter. Over the two-month period, various isolation techniques were mastered and different culturing protocols for tardigrades were established, contributing to the growing knowledge of tardigrade diversity in central India.

Microscopic view of a tardigrade isolated from water or lichen samples using a foldscope.

Introduction

Tardigrades, commonly known as water bears, are microscopic invertebrates known for their remarkable resilience and survival capabilities in extreme environments. This research project focused on discovering new tardigrade species in the central region of India, specifically around Bhopal, where tardigrade biodiversity remains largely unexplored.

The integration of Foldscope portable microscopy represents a significant advancement in tardigrade field research. These paper-based microscopes enable real-time field observations and preliminary species identification without requiring expensive laboratory equipment. The Foldscope's 140× magnification capacity provides sufficient resolution for initial morphological characterization of tardigrades, facilitating immediate documentation of potential novel species.

Foldscope paper microscopes used for field sampling and microscopic observation of pond water, lichens, and tree bark.

Materials and Methods

Habitat Sampling Strategy

The research employed systematic sampling across five distinct microhabitat types representative of central India's ecosystem diversity:

Pond Water Environments: Freshwater aquatic systems with varying pH and nutrient profiles, typically harboring streamlined aquatic tardigrade species adapted to constant moisture conditions. These environments often support species from the genera Hypsibius and Thulinius, known for their aquatic specializations.

Moss Communities: Terrestrial bryophyte assemblages in moist, shaded areas providing desiccation-resistant microenvironments. Moss-dwelling tardigrades typically exhibit enhanced anhydrobiotic capabilities, allowing survival during periodic drying cycles.

Tree Bark Microhabitats: Crevices and rough surfaces of various tree species providing protected niches with stable moisture conditions. These environments typically support robust tardigrade morphotypes with enhanced adhesive capabilities for substrate attachment.

Leaf Litter Systems: Decomposing organic matter on forest floors creating detrital microenvironments rich in bacterial and fungal food sources. Tardigrades in these habitats often exhibit specialized feeding adaptations for processing decomposed organic matter.

Isolation and Cultivation Techniques

The study implemented standardized isolation protocols following established methodologies for tardigrade research. Key techniques included:

Direct Microscopic Extraction: Individual specimens were isolated using micropipettes under stereo microscopy, ensuring minimal contamination and maximum specimen viability. This approach allows for single-specimen DNA extraction protocols essential for molecular characterization.

Laboratory Culture Establishment: Isolated specimens were maintained in spring water with algal food sources, following protocols that support long-term tardigrade cultivation. Successful cultures enable reproductive studies and life cycle observations critical for taxonomic characterization.

Foldscope Microscopy Protocols

Field Documentation: The Foldscope enabled immediate morphological assessment during field collection, allowing real-time identification of potentially novel specimens. Key morphological features documented included body segmentation, claw morphology, pharyngeal armature, and cuticular patterns.

Digital Image Capture: Smartphone integration with Foldscope systems facilitated high-quality digital documentation of specimens for later detailed analysis. These images serve as voucher specimens for taxonomic verification and morphological comparison.

Results and Discussion

The potential novel species identified require comprehensive morphological analysis and molecular characterization to confirm their taxonomic status. Modern tardigrade taxonomy increasingly relies on integrative approaches combining morphology, molecular phylogenetics, and ecological data for robust species descriptions.

Advanced Techniques and Future Directions

Molecular Characterization Protocols: Future work should implement single-specimen DNA extraction protocols specifically developed for tardigrade research. These methods enable phylogenetic analysis using 18S ribosomal RNA and COI gene sequences, providing definitive species identification and evolutionary relationships.

Ultralow input sequencing methods allow complete genome characterization from individual tardigrade specimens, revolutionizing taxonomic resolution and enabling discovery of cryptic species morphologically indistinguishable but genetically distinct.

Ecological Monitoring Applications: Tardigrades serve as bioindicators of environmental quality, with species composition reflecting ecosystem health and pollution impacts. The established protocols from this study could support long-term ecological monitoring programs assessing environmental change impacts on microscopic biodiversity.

Applications and Future Directions

Biodiversity Conservation: Documenting and preserving tardigrade diversity as indicators of ecosystem health and environmental change.

Astrobiology Research: Studying tardigrade survival mechanisms for insights into life in extreme environments beyond Earth.

Biomimetic Applications: Learning from tardigrade adaptations to develop new technologies for preservation and protection of biological materials.

Citizen Science Initiatives: Expanding tardigrade research through educational programs using accessible microscopy technologies like Foldscope.

Conclusions

This internship project successfully demonstrated the feasibility of comprehensive tardigrade biodiversity surveys in underexplored regions of India using accessible microscopy technology and standardized sampling protocols. The discovery of potential novel species highlights the significant taxonomic knowledge gaps remaining in Indian tardigrade fauna and the urgent need for systematic biodiversity documentation.

The integration of Foldscope microscopy with traditional taxonomic methods provides a cost-effective approach for tardigrade research, particularly valuable in resource-limited settings and field-based studies. This methodology could be scaled up for regional biodiversity assessments and citizen science initiatives involving student researchers.

This work contributes to the growing recognition of India as a tardigrade biodiversity hotspot requiring intensive taxonomic investigation to fully document its microscopic fauna. The methodological frameworks established during this internship provide templates for systematic tardigrade surveys across India's diverse ecological regions, supporting national biodiversity conservation goals and international taxonomic databases.
