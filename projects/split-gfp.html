Developing a Split-GFP complementation based sensor for canonical WNT signaling activity in Drosophila S2R+ cells

Key takeaway

This study successfully developed a split-GFP complementation biosensor system that enables real-time visualization of canonical Wnt signaling activation through nuclear import of β-catenin (Armadillo) in Drosophila S2R cells, providing a robust tool for understanding Wnt pathway dynamics.

Abstract

Canonical Wnt signaling plays a critical role in development, tissue homeostasis, and disease pathogenesis. Nuclear import of β-catenin represents a hallmark of canonical Wnt pathway activation, yet existing methods for visualizing this process face significant limitations. This study presents a novel split-GFP complementation biosensor system for real-time detection of canonical Wnt signaling activity in Drosophila S2R cells. Using Gibson assembly, we successfully constructed fusion proteins combining Armadillo (Drosophila β-catenin homolog) with N-terminal GFP fragments and nuclear localization signals with C-terminal GFP fragments. The system demonstrates functional GFP reconstitution only upon nuclear translocation of Armadillo following Wnt pathway activation by Wingless protein or lithium chloride treatment. Confocal microscopy analysis confirmed dose and time-dependent nuclear localization of the Armadillo-GFP complex, validating the biosensor's effectiveness for quantitative Wnt signaling studies.

Introduction

The transformation of a single fertilized egg into a complex multicellular organism represents one of biology's most remarkable achievements. This process requires precise spatiotemporal control of gene expression, cell fate determination, and morphogenetic movements coordinated by sophisticated signaling networks. Among these regulatory systems, Wnt signaling pathways occupy a central position in controlling developmental patterning, tissue homeostasis, and stem cell maintenance.

The canonical Wnt signaling pathway centers on the regulation of β-catenin stability and nuclear localization. In the inactive state, cytoplasmic β-catenin undergoes continuous degradation through a destruction complex comprising Adenomatous Polyposis Coli (APC), Axin1, Casein Kinase 1 (CK1), and Glycogen Synthase Kinase 3 (GSK3). Sequential phosphorylation by CK1 and GSK3 marks β-catenin for ubiquitination and proteasomal degradation, preventing nuclear accumulation.

Aberrant Wnt signaling contributes to numerous human diseases, including various cancers, neurodegeneration, metabolic disorders, and developmental abnormalities. Understanding Wnt pathway dynamics therefore holds significant therapeutic potential for treating these conditions.

Materials and Methods

This study aimed to develop a split-GFP complementation system for visualizing canonical Wnt signaling activation through β-catenin nuclear import in Drosophila S2R cells. S2R cells were selected as they express Frizzled receptors and respond to Wingless signaling, making them an ideal model system for Wnt pathway studies.

The experimental approach involved fusing Armadillo (Drosophila β-catenin) with N-terminal GFP fragments (nGFP) and nuclear localization signals with C-terminal GFP fragments (cGFP). Gibson assembly facilitated construct generation due to its efficiency in joining multiple DNA fragments with overlapping sequences.

Armadillo and nGFP fragments were amplified using primers containing complementary overhangs for seamless assembly. The pAct vector was linearized by restriction digestion with KpnI and XbaI enzymes, removing the existing insert while preserving the constitutive promoter.

S2R cells were maintained at 28°C in Complete Schneider's Drosophila Medium and subcultured to densities of 2-4 × 10^6 cells/ml. Transfections were performed using Effectene Transfection Reagent with 50 ng/μl plasmid concentrations.

Results and Discussion

Initial experiments confirmed split-GFP system functionality in S2R cells. Co-transfection of pAct-NLS-nGFP and pAct-NLS-cGFP plasmids produced robust nuclear fluorescence, demonstrating successful GFP reconstitution. Individual fragments showed no detectable fluorescence, confirming the requirement for both components.

Gibson assembly successfully generated the pAct-Armadillo-nGFP construct, as confirmed by PCR amplification showing expected fragment sizes: Armadillo (2.5 kb) and nGFP (628 bp). Colony screening identified positive clones through successive PCR rounds using different primer combinations.

Without Wnt pathway activation, the Armadillo-nGFP construct remained predominantly cytoplasmic when co-expressed with either cytoplasmic cGFP (pAct-CYTO-cGFP) or nuclear cGFP (pAct-NLS-cGFP). This localization pattern confirmed that Armadillo requires external signaling for nuclear translocation.

Wingless protein overexpression induced dramatic nuclear accumulation of the Armadillo-nGFP fusion protein, resulting in bright fluorescence when co-expressed with NLS-cGFP. This response confirmed successful pathway activation and validated the biosensor's ability to detect canonical Wnt signaling.

LiCl treatment provided dose- and time-dependent nuclear translocation of Armadillo-nGFP. 4 mM LiCl for 1-2 hours produced partial nuclear localization, while 8 mM LiCl for 2 hours achieved complete nuclear translocation. These results established optimal conditions for pathway activation and demonstrated the biosensor's quantitative capabilities.

Applications and Future Directions

The split-GFP biosensor enables numerous research applications including drug screening, pathway component analysis, and developmental studies. The system could facilitate identification of novel Wnt modulators and investigation of pathway crosstalk mechanisms.

Given Wnt signaling's role in cancer and other diseases, the biosensor could support therapeutic development. The system might enable screening for compounds that normalize aberrant Wnt signaling in disease contexts.

Future developments could incorporate inducible promoter systems for better temporal control. CRISPR-mediated genome integration could generate stable cell lines expressing the biosensor under endogenous promoters.

Conclusions

This study successfully developed and validated a split-GFP complementation biosensor for canonical Wnt signaling detection in Drosophila S2R cells. Key achievements include:

- Technical Success: Gibson assembly enabled efficient construction of functional Armadillo-nGFP fusion proteins with preserved biological activity.

- Biological Validation: The biosensor demonstrated specific response to canonical Wnt pathway activation through both Wingless protein overexpression and LiCl treatment.

- Quantitative Capabilities: Dose- and time-dependent responses to LiCl treatment confirmed the system's utility for quantitative pathway analysis.

- Methodological Advantages: Split-GFP complementation provided superior sensitivity and simplicity compared to alternative approaches.

The successful development of this biosensor represents a significant advance in Wnt signaling research methodology. The system's ability to provide real-time, quantitative assessment of canonical pathway activation addresses longstanding limitations in the field.
